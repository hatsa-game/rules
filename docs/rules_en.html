Hatsa – Rules

English version

1Objective

The goal of Hatsa is to score as many points as possible by correctly predicting how many tricks you will win in each round.

2Hatsa in a nutshell

The game consists of multiple rounds. The number of cards per round changes (increasing each round and then decreasing again). In each round, players compete for tricks using their cards.

The deck consists of 56 cards. Each suit contains cards with values 1 through 9 and E, D, C, B, A. Letter cards outrank number cards, with E being the lowest and A the highest value.

3Scorecard example

4Getting started

Before the game begins, decide:

  • the total number of rounds
  • the number of cards per round

(for example: 2–4–6–8–9–7–5–3–1)

5Dealing

Determine the dealer for the first round by giving each player one face-up card. The player with the lowest card becomes the dealer.

The dealer:

  • deals the correct number of cards to each player
  • turns over the top card of the remaining deck – this determines the Hatsa (trump)
  • gives each player four action cards

6Action cards

One by one, players may choose whether they want to play an action card for this round. Each player must have played three of their four action cards by the end of the game. The player to the left of the dealer starts, followed clockwise.

7Bidding

Each player decides how many tricks they expect to win this round. All players reveal their bids simultaneously by showing fingers (countdown: 1–2–3).

8

If the total number of bids equals the number of cards in the round, the dealer must change (for example: 4-card round, bids: 1–0–1–2 = 4).

9Playing the hand

The player to the left of the dealer leads by playing any card. Players follow clockwise and must follow suit if possible. The highest card of the led suit wins the trick.

If a player cannot follow suit, they may play any other suit or a Hatsa (trump) card. The highest Hatsa card always wins the trick.

10

The winner of a trick leads the next one. This continues until all cards have been played. The round then ends.

11Scoring

  • Exact prediction: 10 points plus 5 points per trick won (e.g. predicted 2, won 2 = 20 points).
  • Incorrect prediction: minus 5 points for each trick difference (e.g. predicted 2, won 3 = −5 points).

Add or subtract the points from each player’s total score after every round.

12End of the game

Once all rounds have been played, the game ends. The player with the highest total score wins.
